Descending Thirds Melody

The “rules” for Descending Thirds Melody are based on the flow of functional harmony and a few melodic intervals.

Functional harmony flows from “subdominant” to “dominant” to “tonic”. In Roman numeral symbols that is I > IV > V. Those chords, in that order, in melodic descending thirds gives the scale degrees ^1 ^6 ^4 ^2 ^7 ^5 ^3 ^1. If those scale degrees are grouped with their corresponding chords, we get ^1 ^6 ^4 (IV) ^4 ^2 ^7 ^5 (V7) ^5 ^3 ^1 (I). This is a useful melodic pattern, because it ensures good harmonic flow. No matter where you start in the scale, if you move in descending melodic third, it always follows good functional harmonic flow. It is found frequently in classical music.

However, the pattern needs a few additional rules to become a practical guide for making melodies.

The line needs to ascend sometimes.

  • The descending third can be inverted for an ascending sixth

The functional progression I > IV > V can be rotated to V > I > IV to reveal it is roots by descending fifths. The root progression in terms of ascent inverts to an ascending fourth. The voice leading of that harmonic pattern involves two chord tones move by one ascending step. Altogether this allows two additional ascending moves...

  • An ascending step
  • An ascending fourth

The interval of a tritone is typically avoided, or at least requires careful handing. For the sake of simplicity we will avoid it. So, moves B > F or F > B are forbidden.

  • The tritone is forbidden

Finally, the melody starts on either ^1 or ^5, continues for several moves, and then forms a cadence. The melody is presented without rhythm nor measures. It is a sort of melodic “skeleton” which could be developed with rhythm and meter, and perhaps embellished, to make an actual melody.
